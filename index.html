About Babel

In 1828, Robin Swift arrives at Oxford University as the ward of the mysterious Professor Lovell. There, he discovers a world of dark academia where translation is not just an academic pursuit, but a form of magic that powers the British Empire's dominance.

At Babel, Oxford's Royal Institute of Translation, students manipulate silver bars through the precise art of translation, creating magical effects that fuel Britain's industrial and military might. But this power comes at a terrible cost.

As Robin delves deeper into the world of silver-working, he uncovers the violent foundation upon which the Institute—and the Empire itself—is built. When revolution comes to Oxford, Robin must choose between the world that has made him and the world he could help create.

Silver Working

The Raw Silver

All silver begins as a pure bar of unmarked metal, mined from colonies across the globe and shipped back to Britain. In its natural state, silver is nothing more than a conduit — highly conductive, yet inert without the work of language.

Raw silver bars are carefully refined and polished until they gleam with a mirror-like surface. They hold no power of their own, but their very purity allows them to store the linguistic paradoxes that fuel Babel’s magic.

It is said that even before engraving, silver seems to hum faintly in silence, as if waiting to be spoken into.

The Engraved Silver

The bar’s true power emerges when it is engraved with word pairs from different languages. These inscriptions capture the gaps in translation — the subtle differences between meaning that no language can fully bridge. The silver stores this unspoken tension and releases it as usable energy.

On its own, an engraved silver bar glows faintly, resonating with the words etched into its surface. But when attached to an object — a lamp post, a carriage, even an entire building — the bar channels its energy into practical function: brighter light, smoother travel, or reinforced strength.

Over time, engraved silver is not eternal. Should the surface become damaged, corroded, or worn, the inscriptions lose clarity, and the paradox unravels. A broken bar falls silent — disposable, no longer able to sustain its magic.

Lifecycle of a Silver Bar

1

Raw Silver

Extracted from deep mines across the empire, raw silver holds immense potential but remains inert. Only when engraved with carefully chosen translation pairs does it awaken its latent resonance. At this stage, it is precious but powerless.

2

Engraving

Translators inscribe words from different languages onto the bar, binding their tension and difference. The gap between meanings releases energy, turning the silver into a conduit of hidden power. Each engraving is unique, suited for specific functions — from heating a kettle to fueling great engines.

3

Active Use

Once embedded into objects — a lamp post, a carriage, a printing press — the engraved bar continuously channels energy, sustaining its effect without fuel or flame. This resonance is subtle yet constant: a lamp glows brighter than any oil flame, doors lock with unseen strength, and carriages move smoother than mere wood and wheel allow. However, each bar hums with finite life, slowly wearing as its engraved words exhaust their tension.

4

Maintenance

Every 6–8 months — sometimes longer if rarely used — the bars must be re-engraved or replaced. Translators return them to Babel’s workshops, where the words are refreshed to restore their strength. Neglect leads to faltering energy and eventual silence.

5

Disposability

When a silver bar cracks or the engravings wear beyond recovery, it becomes inert. A spent bar cannot be revived — its once-bright resonance fading to dull metal. At this stage, it is discarded or melted down, a relic of Babel’s unending hunger for silver.
